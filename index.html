Here‚Äôs a little Reader‚Äôs Digest about my personal life, so far:

I was born in Yugoslavia, and raised young in Serbia and later Poland. In time for primary school, my family moved to Prague, Czech Republic, where the entirety of my primary schooling was at a British international school. It was during these formative years I played too many sports and wrote poems profusely.

As a young adult I moved to the Netherlands, where I obtained Bachelor and Master degrees in the city of Utrecht, discovering my unwavering interest in biology (or rather, the life sciences, named due to the intersection of traditional biology with the branches of physical sciences such as chemistry and physics), a field underpinned and peppered with complexity and layers of emergence. It was in Utrecht I think I began to refer to my interests as broad and multidisciplinary. As I discovered my aptitude within the life sciences, I found this area to be particularly rich in cross-disciplinary pollination. For me, the exploration of how living systems work - from molecules all the way across systems and organizations to fully-fledged individual organisms, participating in ecospheres and communities, with behaviors and lifestyles to boot - is simply unmatched in fulfillment. My endeavors in the life sciences have allowed me to learn so many investigative and exploratory techniques, about unlikely interconnections across disparate topics, about how problems are solved, how questions are answered, and granted opportunities to work with people from different scientific backgrounds, defined by unique strengths, employing different tools, within diverse research cultures. I‚Äôve found no two biologists truly alike. It simply allows for professional work where resourcefulness, insightfulness and endless joys of learning to mix and co-exist.

After a subsequent homecoming, soul-searching and stint in Prague‚Äôs weird and vibrant art community for a year, I decided to pursue a PhD in molecular biology and neuroscience in Jena, Germany, working simultaneously on many projects involving fruit flies, neurogenetics and olfactory biology at the Max Planck Institute for Chemical Ecology.

I worked many years on studying the sense of smell, and carving out my own niche of understanding what role the poorly-understood non-neuronal cells might play in the sensory process. I became familiar with different sensory systems across the animal kingdom. During these research years, an incidental pandemic swept the globe. Upon infection, most of us were familiarized with its signature symptom: a sudden loss of smell, or anosmia, later confirmed to arise from viral entry into non-neuronal cells in our noses, disrupting our sensory ability to perceive airborne chemicals. I was able to shed some insights and publish a few works describing how non-neuronal cells function and co-operate with neurons, ultimately allowing creatures to smell the world. Research science in this field is a wild ride. I completed my doctorate with flying colors and now pose myself for another wild turn of chance and irony, as I look for the next challenge and playground.

Aside from career, I take on hobbies plentifully. I self-describe as a musician. I play the bass, play in bands and listen to music voraciously. I also enjoy sports and games; I grew up playing football competitively, developed into a chess player, and try to keep up with the kaleidoscope of play and games out there. I love producing film and videos, organizing barbecue parties and relaxing in nature.

Let‚Äôs see where career and life take me! As ever, I remain open to opportunities, job offers and a chat.
