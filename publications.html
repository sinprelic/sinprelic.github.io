as LEAD AUTHOR:

Prelic, Jain et al.
Expression of Drosophila melanogaster V-ATPases in olfactory sensillum support cells.

Published in Insects journal in 2024.
DOI link.

Short summary

Research Article: 13 pages, 7 figures

Context
Sensory systems feature specialized compartments, in which physiological concentrations must be tightly regulated to allow for correct sensory functioning. How this is regulated is not well understood, especially for the sensory system-critical protons and K+.

Our approach
We performed bioinformatic analyses and immunofluorescence microscopy to explore and then pinpoint the expression of proton pumps in the fruit fly sensory system.

Results
We found many proton pump family genes expressed in abundance in the antennal organ. Differential expression analysis of single-cell transcriptomes further revealed likely non-neuronal localization. This was then confirmed at the protein level through molecular tagging of proton pumps in immunohistochemical preparations, showing pumps residing on the non-neuronal cell populations.

Conclusions
The ionic composition of the sensory lymph is likely regulated by support cell-expressed pumps, which shuttle protons into the extracellular milieu. This gradient likely further allows for the enrichment of important ions such as K+ with the use of proton/K+ antiporters situated on support cells that are adjacent to sensory neurons.

S. Prelic
Non-canonical sensory biology of antennal cells in insect olfaction.

Published in Thuringian University & State Library (ThULB) in 2024.
DOI link.

Short summary

Dissertation: 261 pages, 13 figures, 3 embedded manuscripts

Contents
Here I describe the state-of-the-art concerning peripheral olfactory systems in insects. I exhaustively identify and review non-neuronal processes and constitutents of olfactory organs as crucial (though under-explored) elements.
To more completely understand the neuroscience of chemical sensing, I then explore several 'non-canonical' biological mechanisms in the antenna. I present original research articles, each addressing:

  • Intercellular interactions between neuronal and non-neuronal cells. I demonstrate coupled activity between these cell-types during sensory stimulation,
  • Paracrine signaling (messenger-mediated cell-cell communication), exploring signaling pathways and their effects on potentiating sensory processes, and
  • Juxtacrine signaling (contact-dependent communication), showing the presence of gap junctions within insect olfactory organs.

I also propose a wide range of novel considerations, hypotheses and practical research directions for future exploration of animal sensory systems, given surprising, unreported parallels I note exist among the animal kingdom.

Prelic et al.
Innexin expression and localization in the Drosophila antenna indicate gap junction or hemichannel involvement in antennal chemosensory sensilla.

Published in Cell and Tissue Research in 2024.
DOI link.

Short summary

Research Article: 28 pages, 7 figures, 4 supplementary figures

Context
The structure of olfactory systems is highly conserved: sensory neurons and adjacent cells are closely positioned and form tight compartments. Whether gap junctions (direct cell-cell contacts) interconnect the cells or compartments is unclear.

Results
Here, I show that innexins (which form hemichannels and gap junctions in invertebrates) are abundantly expressed in antennae of adult flies. I discover innexin-specific patterns of expression and localization, with a majority strongly localizing to glial and other non-neuronal cells, shown with immunostaining and bioinformatic analyses. In a neuron backfilling (dye coupling) case study, I show no such interconnection exists on sensory neurons, implying that such channels are exclusive to non-neuronal cells.

Conclusions
Gap junctions likely couple support cells among each other. I discuss how coupling of sensillum microenvironments or compartments may facilitate odor sensing and sensillum homeostasis.

Prelic et al.
Modulation of the NO-cGMP pathway has no effect on olfactory responses in the Drosophila antenna.

Published in Frontiers in Cellular Neuroscience in 2023.
DOI link.

Short summary

Research Article: 24 pages, 6 figures, 5 supplementary figures

Context & Approach
NO signaling is a ubiquitous mechanism by which cell activity can be potentiated. Here, I use optical and electrophysiological techniques in live antennal tissue to investigate whether nitric oxide (NO) signaling functions in the olfactory organs of insects. I first demonstrate the presence of nitric oxide signaling machinery in antennal tissue. Next, by applying various modulators of the NO-cGMP pathway in open tissue, I show that olfactory responses are unaffected by a wide panel of inhibitors and activators of this pathway, over short and long timescales. I then examine the action of the cyclic nucleotide family of messengers, previously shown to modulate olfactory receptor functionality, and find that - contrary to expectation - various kinds of applications of cGMP have no effect on olfactory responses in vivo in microinjection, calcium imaging and single sensillum recording experiments. This is differentiated from cAMP effects which confer effects on neuron responses.

Conclusions
In living animals, NO signals may play no role as regulators of olfactory transduction in insects, though may play other physiological roles in insect sensory organs. Several explanations and hypotheses are posed.

Prelic et al.
Functional interaction between Drosophila olfactory sensory neurons and their support cells.

Published in Frontiers in Cellular Neuroscience in 2022.
DOI link.

Short summary

Research Article: 23 pages, 6 figures, 5 supplementary figures

Context
Insects smell the world using antennae. These organs house a vast variety of olfactory sensory neurons (OSNs) that innervate hair-like structures called sensilla where odor detection takes place. The antenna also hosts various non-neuronal support cell types, which are widely conserved in evolution. While neurons are well-studied, very little is known about the functional roles support cells play.

Results
I use a combination of optical and electrophysiological tools in Drosophila. I first characterize the distribution of various supporting cells using genetic markers. I then show these non-neuronal cells are activated during sensory stimulation, in ex vivo antennal preparations genetically encoding Ca2+ and novel K+ indicators. I observe acute responses but distinct differences in Ca2+ and K+ fluxes between support cell types. Lastly, I 'knock out' support cells and show specific impairments in odor sensing in living flies.

Conclusions
This work demonstrates support cells play more active, responsive roles during odor processing. Such functional interactions between neurons and non-neuronal cells may underlie the extraordinary ability of insect olfactory systems to dynamically and sensitively discriminate between odors in the turbulent sensory landscape of insect flight.

as SUPPORTING AUTHOR:

Mokry et al. (2014). Epigenetic regulation of immune tolerance in intestinal epithelium. Journal of Crohn's and Colitis. Oral presentation. DOI link.
Meddens et al. (2019). Stem cells are the principal intestinal epithelial responders to bacterial antigens. SSRN Electronic Journal, Cell Stem Cell. DOI link.

I was involved in the initial stages performing exploratory qRT-PCR experiments establishing the basis for the research story, and assisted in proofing the first article drafts. My results showed relative changes in mRNA expression of inflammatory markers in intestinal (duodenum, ileum and colon) organoid samples following bacterial antigen exposure. This result served as springboard for further investigations demonstrating that intestinal stem cells (in contrast to enterocytes) are local immune response mediators in the large intestinal epithelium, involving the WNT pathway.

The complete work was eventually published many years later here.
