Doctoral Dissertation: Non-canonical sensory biology of antennal cells in insect olfaction

Year: 2024
Doctoral advisors: Prof. Bill S. Hansson, Prof. Stefan H. Heinemann
Supervision: PD Dr. Dieter Wicher, Dr. Sofía Lavista Llanos
Committee: Faculty of Biological Sciences, Friedrich Schiller University Jena

This work is a treatise on the underexplored aspects of insect olfaction. It focuses on non-neuronal cells and mechanisms beyond that of olfactory receptor-mediated sensory reception.
Included: an extensive review, several original research manuscripts, and a detailed discussion on the present and future state of the field.

MSc Thesis: Physiological functions of abundant extracellular chaperones

Full title: Physiological functions of abundant extracellular chaperones: involvement of secreted chaperones in proteostasis, immunity and protein deposition diseases
Year: 2016
Supervision: Dr. Martina Radli, Prof. Stefan Rudiger
Location: Dept. Cellular Protein Chemistry, Faculty of Science, Utrecht University

In this work I review disconnected literature concerning molecular chaperones (proteins that sequester and help other proteins fold correctly). In particular, I focus on the set of chaperones known to function outside the cell. Such chaperones are extremely abundant in bodily fluids and implicated in health and disease, but remain poorly characterized. This work aims to be a first unifying review comparing (dis)similarities among them. I argue that extracellular chaperones present as attractive, imperative avenues for further biomedical research, and as targets for future medical interventions.

MSc Research project: Microtubule/MTOC dynamics in sprouting angiogenesis

Full title: Microtubule involvement in sprouting angiogenesis: Role of microtubule minus end-binding protein CAMSAP2 and MTOC organelles in endothelial spheroid model system
Type: Master Research Project
Year: 2015-2016
Supervision: Dr. Maud J. Martin, Prof. Anna Akhmanova
Location: Cellular Dynamics (Akhmanova) lab, Dept. Cell Biology, Faculty of Science, Utrecht University

In this year-long project, I used endothelial spheroids made from human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVECs) to model and induce blood vessel outgrowth (i.e. sprouting angiogenesis). In particular, I focused on studying which role microtubule, microtubule end-binding proteins and microtubule-organizing centers (MTOCs) such as the Golgi apparatus and centrosomes have in the sprouting process. Furthermore, with RNAi, I knocked out CAMSAP2, a microtubule minus end-stabilizing protein, and showed abberant sprouting and Golgi organization. I also performed a lot of cytological quantitation using widefield and matrix-embedded live imaging, including counting mitochondria, looking for patterning in elements of the cytoskeleton and organelles.

MSc Research project: Developing STARR-seq for the human genome

Full title: Application of STARR-Seq to the human genome: a genome-wide DNA regulatory region screen
Type: Master research Project
Year: 2014-2015
Supervision: Dr. Michal Mokry
Location: Pediatric Gastroenterology, Laboratory of Translational Immunology, Wilhelmina Children’s Hospital, University Medical Center Utrecht

For over a year, in this project I adapted a new high-throughput technique (STARR-seq) through which we can identify and measure the activity of DNA regulatory elements (e.g. enhancers). Specifically, the method was limited to work only on smaller genomes and required exhaustive cloning efforts to obtain enough DNA of interest at sufficient library complexity and depth for assaying. I discovered a way to circumvent this requirement by preparing suffient “input” DNA in vitro. I show the transcriptional activity readout is of sufficient complexity, covering the entire human genome in terms of readout information; additionally, I compare the result with golden standard assay results (luciferase reporter assays), showing that the novel adapted methodology is cheaper, quicker, and now viable for human (and similarly large) genomes. Reading transcriptional activity en masse allows to identify and quantitate DNA regulatory element activity in a single experimental run, which in turn helps discover the critical roles DNA elements play in cell type specification and disease pathogenesis.

BSc Thesis: Fungal physiology of thermophiles on complex substrates

Full title: Saccharification capacity of the industrially relevant thermophile Myceliophthora heterothallica by comparing parental strains with progeny selected ofr growth on spruce biomass
Year: 2013
Supervision: Dr. Joost van den Brink
Location: Westerdijk Fungal Biodiversity Institute (formerly: CBS-KNAW Fungal Biodiversity Centre)

The thesis subject includes third-gen (lignocellulosic) biofuel production and the potential use of thermophilic fungi in degrading complex plant matter as a means of improving biofuel production. In this context, I present experimental evidence and a research report involving testing of the sugar-releasing capacity in strains and growth-selected progeny of industrially-relevant thermophiles, grown on 7 complex plant biomasses. Experiments involve growth profiling of thermophilic, filamentous fungi cultured in both solid and liquid phase, sexually crossing them, selecting catabolic strains, and testing their CAZyme activity and degree of enzyme production in various ways. Essentially, the aim of this project was to help further reveal whether biofuel generation from non-food crops is industrially viable.
