Publications

As lead author:

Prelic, Jain et al.
Expression of Drosophila melanogaster V-ATPases in olfactory sensillum support cells.

Published in Insects journal in 2024.

Link Supplementary data (FigShare)

Summary (Research Article: 13 pages, 7 figures)

We perform bioinformatic analyses and immunofluorescence microscopy to explore and then pinpoint the expression of proton pumps in the fruit fly olfactory system. We hypothesize such pumps are situated on non-neuronal cells, and are involved in the critical regulation of proton and K+ concentrations necessary for correct sensory functioning.

We discover abundant expression of many genes composing the proton pump family in Drosophila antennae, and further localize these to support cells of antennal sensilla by analysis of cell type classes in single-cell transcriptomes, as well as showing their localization via immunolabeling in antennal sections. We demonstrate and further discuss mechanisms by which extracellular ionic compositions are maintained, and how known proton/K+ antiporters may then facilitate sensory function in animals.

Prelic, S.
Non-canonical sensory biology of antennal cells in insect olfaction.

Published in Thuringian University & State Library (ThULB) in 2024.

Link

Summary (Dissertation: 261 pages, 13 figures, 3 embedded manuscripts)

I identify and review non-neuronal processes and constituents of olfactory organs as crucial (though under-explored) elements of insect olfactory systems. I then set out to investigate several ‘non-canonical’ mechanisms in insect antennae, an area that remains entirely unexplored. I present original research articles on:

  • intercellular interactions between neuronal and non-neuronal cells. I demonstrate coupled activity between these cell types during sensory activity,
  • paracrine signaling (messenger-mediated cell-cell communication), exploring signaling pathways and their effects on potentiating sensory processes,
  • juxtacrine signaling (contact-dependent communication), showing the presence of gap junctions within insect olfactory organs.

Given this budding subject and field of research, I also propose a wide range of novel considerations, hypotheses and practical research directions for future exploration of animal sensory systems, especially given notable (though unreported) parallels among various sensory systems

Prelic et al.
Innexin expression and localization in the Drosophila antenna indicate gap junction or hemichannel involvement in antennal chemosensory sensilla.

Published in Cell and Tissue Research in 2024.

Link Supplementary data

Summary (Research Article: 28 pages, 7 figures, 4 supplementary figures)

Olfactory system structures are highly conserved, with sensory neurons and adjacent cells closely apositioned, forming tight and encosed compartments. Whether gap junctions (direct cell-cell contacts) interconnect the cells or compartments is unclear. Here I show innexins (which form hemichannels and gap junctions in invertebrates) are abundantly expressed in antennae of adult flies.

I discover innexin-specific patterns of expression and localization, with a majority strongly localizing to glial and other non-neuronal cells, shown with immunostaining and bioinformatic analyses. In a neuron backfilling (dye coupling) case study, I show no such interconnection exists on sensory neurons, implying that such channels are likely exclusive to non-neuronal cells. Based on these observations, I discuss how coupling of sensillum microenvironments or compartments may facilitate odor sensing as well as sensillum homeostasis.

Prelic et al.
Modulation of the NO‑cGMP pathway has no effect on olfactory responses in the Drosophila antenna.

Published in Frontiers in Cellular Neuroscience in 2023.

Link Supplementary data

Summary (Research Article: 24 pages, 6 figures, 5 supplementary figures)

Nitric oxide (NO) signaling is a ubiquitous mechanism by which cells activity may be potentiated. Here, I use optical and electrophysiological techniques in live antennal tissue to investigate whether NO signaling functions in the olfactory organs of insects. I first demonstrate the presence of NO signaling machinery in antennal tissue. Next, by applying various modulators of the NO-cGMP pathway in open tissue, I show that olfactory responses are unaffected by a wide panel of inhibitors and activators of this pathway, over short and long timescales. This is contrary to expectations, as insect olfactory receptors have been shown to be modulated by cGMP in vitro.

I then examine the action of the cyclic nucleotide family of messengers, previously shown to modulate olfactory receptor functionality, and find that - contrary to expectation - various kinds of applications of cGMP have no effect on olfactory responses in vivo in microinjection, calcium imaging and single sensillum recording experiments. This is differentiated from cAMP effects which confer effects on neuron responses.

In living animals, NO signals may play no role as regulators of olfactory transduction in insects, though may play other physiological roles in insect sensory organs. Several explanations and hypotheses are posed.

Prelic et al.
Functional interaction between Drosophila olfactory sensory neurons and their support cells.

Published in Frontiers in Cellular Neuroscience in 2022.

Link Supplementary data (EDMOND)

Summary (Research Article: 23 pages, 6 figures, 5 supplementary figures)

Abstract: Insects detect volatile chemicals using antennae, which house a vast variety of olfactory sensory neurons (OSNs) that innervate hair-like structures called sensilla where odor detection takes place. In addition to OSNs, the antenna also hosts various support cell types. These include the triad of trichogen, tormogen, and thecogen support cells that lie adjacent to their respective OSNs. The arrangement of OSN supporting cells occurs stereotypically for all sensilla and is widely conserved in evolution. While insect chemosensory neurons have received considerable attention, little is known about the functional significance of the cells that support them. For instance, it remains unknown whether support cells play an active role in odor detection, or only passively contribute to homeostasis, e.g., by maintaining sensillum lymph composition. To investigate the functional interaction between OSNs and support cells, we used optical and electrophysiological approaches in Drosophila.

First, we characterized the distribution of various supporting cells using genetic markers. By means of an ex vivo antennal preparation and genetically-encoded Ca2+ and K+ indicators, we then studied the activation of these auxiliary cells during odor presentation in adult flies. We observed acute responses and distinct differences in Ca+ and K+ fluxes between support cell types. Finally, we observed alterations in OSN responses upon thecogen cell ablation in mature adults. Upon inducible ablation of thecogen cells, we notice a gain in mechanical responsiveness to mechanical stimulations during single-sensillum recording, but a lack of change to the neuronal resting activity. Taken together, these results demonstrate that support cells play a more active and responsive role during odor processing than previously thought. Our observations thus reveal that support cells functionally interact with OSNs and may be important for the extraordinary ability of insect olfactory systems to dynamically and sensitively discriminate between odors in the turbulent sensory landscape of insect flight.

As supporting author:

Mokry et al. (2014). Epigenetic regulation of immune tolerance in intestinal epithelium. Journal of Crohn’s and Colitis. DOI link.

Meddens et al. (2019). Stem cells are the principal intestinal epithelial responders to bacterial antigens. SSRN Electronic Journal: Cell Stem Cell. DOI link.

I was involved in initial stages performing exploratory qRT-PCR experiments establishing the research story basis, and assisted in proofing first drafts of the paper. The work was delayed as it went through several lead author changes.

My results showed relative changes in mRNA expression of inflammatory markers in intestinal (duodenum, ileum and colon) samples following bacterial antigen exposure. This result served as springboard for further investigations demonstrating that intestinal stem cells (in contrast to enterocytes) are local immune response mediators in the large intestinal epithelium, involving the WNT pathway.

The complete work was eventually published many years later here.

Poster example

A showcase of experimental results from 2021 involving live Ca2+ imaging and custom K+ imaging of both neuronal and non-neuronal cell activity during sensory (odor) stimulation, and electrophysiological recordings of sensory responses in flies where olfactory support cells are intact or absent. This was achieved via cell type-targeted, heat-induced ablation using the GAL4/UAS binary expression system, post-developmentally, to control for complex confoundments.

In 2019, at the Annual Institute Symposium of the Max Planck Institute for Chemical Ecology, I presented a poster entitled Revealing fine details in olfactory sensilla: single-nucleus RNA-seq and the role of support cells in olfaction, showcasing experimental results involving the first-ever single-cell transcriptomic dataset of an insect antenna I made using nuclei isolated from Drosophila melanogaster, and sequenced using the microfluidic droplet-based 10XGenomics platform. The poster also included neuron backfilling and dye tracing results, revealing ultrastructures in insect sensilla newly observed with confocal fluorescence imaging.
